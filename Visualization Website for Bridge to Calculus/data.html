Data

The data for MCAS scores come from an official website, the Massachusetts Department of Elementary and Secondary Education. We extracted our desired data (Math scores by demographic for selected years 2006-2019) by hand and constructed a json file from it. For NAEP, we received the data from our professor of DS4200 (Information Presentation and Visualization), Prof. Michelle Borkin. Some of this data included scores from selected years between 2005-2019 also with demographic breakdown, which is what we desired.

The datasets contain quantitative and categorical data. The quantitative data is the actual percentage of students "Meeting or Exceeding Expectations", which we define as "At or Above Proficiency" to maintain consistency with Raj's 2007 presentation. The demographics (Asian, White, Black, Hispanic) is a categorical variable, and also the grade (e.g. 4th grade) is categorical. The Year is ordinal data.

Data Clean-up Process and Challenges

As mentioned above, our group used the MCAS data from an official government website and received the NAEP data directly from our instructor. However, not all of the data was relevant to the goal of this project since we want to concentrate on the math scores. So, we had to filter our data manually and look through all the sheets to prune down to only the data related to math scores. We also had to filter for only Boston for the MCAS data.

We filtered the MCAS data within the website by using its interactive filtering option, since the website contains not only math scores but also scores of other subjects such as English and Science. Afterwards, we filtered the data by the year and recorded the score data for Boston, by demographic level, and by 4th and 8th grade. This gave us Math scores that covered our main deliverables. For the NAEP scores, it was already cleaned and formatted in a copy-pastable way in a supplied spreadsheet format. So, we extracted the yearly data for the national level as well as for MA, for the available grade (12th). This gave us the Math scores for NAEP over available years for the national and state level with a demographic breakdown.

We also faced several problems during the process of collecting data from our partner organization. Firstly, we missed data for 2020 because the tests were cancelled due to the COVID-19. Secondly, our group was not supplied with the detailed data of the students who experienced the help of Bridge of Calculus, which meant that our analysis could not directly show the impact of the work of the organization. In other words, if we could have data to show the improvement of grades of the students, the visualizations could become more specific to BtC and persuasive for the intended stakeholders (funders, parents, and students). There was also no MCAS data for 2015 and 2016 as an alternative test was used for those years.
