Interview

As a group we met twice and communicated several times about the expectations and the full reasoning behind the need for visualizations. She explained that BTC actually grew out of a 2007 presentation she gave in which line charts visualized the math scores over the time period 1998-2005. The visuals showed many things, especially the lag of the Black and Hispanic demographic behind the White and Asian. Worse, the gap was widening as students grew older, which of course impacts the calibre of universities they can attend and results in a cyclic effect. During our conversations with Professor Raj, she explained how no data visualizations have been produced since and she would like to revisit the data. This was to give a sense of progress (if any) and underscore the need for the work BTC does. In particular, the key stakeholders she told us to target were funders. Another expectation on us was to produce simple, easy to consume visualizations. Raj said very clearly that she is not a fan of complex things and would prefer the work to be limited to known chart types, such as line charts and bar charts.

Overall, our task was essentially to revisit the data and produce visualizations related to them which drove insights. We wanted to learn about how Blacks and Hispanics have improved with respect to the other demographics, if at all. We wanted to learn if MA students are above the national average, and in parituclar which race of students. There are many aspects to question, and our goal was to encourage this curiosity through the interactive features we implemented.
